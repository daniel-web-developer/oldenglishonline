Weak Verbs Overview

Weak verbs are the largest category of Old English verbs. They are considered grammatically weak as they form their preterite by adding a dental suffix (-d or -t). For example, -de in the singular and -don in the plural as seen in the figure below. Weak verbs are subdivided into three sub-classes depending on the ending of their infinitive.

Class I weak verbs have an infinitive ending in -an or -rian. Third-person present singular ends in -eþ, and present plural ends in -aþ, while the past is formed using ‐de, or ‐te.

Class II weak verbs have an infinitive ending in -ian (except when preceded by ‘r’ as -rian verbs are generally Class I). Their third-person present singular ending is -aþ, like Class I plurals. Class II present plurals end in -iaþ and the preterite suffix is preceded by an -o.

Class III weak verbs have an infinitive ending in -an and now vowel before the dental suffix. They are more unpredictable in their conjugation and often combine features of the first two weak classes. There are only four Class III verbs: habban - to have , libban - to live, seċġan - to say and hyċġan - to think.
