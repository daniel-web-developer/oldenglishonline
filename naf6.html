Strong Nouns Overview

To recap what we have learned, nouns can have strength, case, gender and number. All these factors impact how a noun declines.

Strong nouns have many inflection variants and generally end in a consonant. However, weak nouns - which we will examine in a later module - have few inflectional variants and generally end in a vowel.

Strong nouns are subdivided up into three grammatical categories called ‘genders’. These categories are purely grammatical and are determined on morphological grounds rather than referential ones.

The case of a noun is determined by its function in a sentence. A subject takes the nominative, a direct object takes the accusative, a possessive takes the genitive and an indirect object takes the dative.

Lastly, nouns decline differently depending on whether they are singular or plural.

Variant Conjugations

Disyllabic nouns

Nouns with two syllables, or 'dysllabic nouns', lose the unstressed vowel of their second syllable when an inflectional ending adds a syllable. We saw this with the feminine noun, ceaster, which loses its second 'e' when conjugated. This is known as syncopation and the rule applies across all genders.

Nouns ending in a vowel

Nouns which end in a vowel (except u) are generally weak. However, there are a handful of exceptions, and when a strong noun ends in a vowel, like ende - end, wite - punishment, or clawu - claw, they usually drop their vowel when adding a suffix.

Nouns with a stem ending in 'h'

Nouns that have a stem ending in 'h' lose it when conjugated. This can happen one of two ways.

If the noun ends in a consonant + h, like mearh - horse, wealh - foreigner and feorh - life, the noun loses the ‘h’ when conjugated.

If the noun ends in a vowel + h, like scoh - shoe and feoh - cattle/wealth, the word loses the ‘h’ and the unaccented vowel when conjugated. However, you should note that feoh only has a singular form, much like the modern words 'cattle' and 'wealth' do not have plural forms.

Nouns with 'æ' in the stem

Monosyllabic nouns with a short 'æ' in the stem, such as hwæl - whale, fæt - cup, and dæg - day, undergo a sound-change to 'a' when conjugated in the plural. This is because the plural suffixes contain the back vowels 'a' and 'u'. Words with a long 'æ', such as dæd - deed, læcce - leech, or dæl - part retain their 'æ' in the plural.
