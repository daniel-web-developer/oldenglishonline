Prehistory

Proto Indo European is the linguistic reconstruction of the common ancestor of all Indo-European languages, including Old English. PIE is estimated to have been spoken as a single language from 4500 BC to 2500 BC and all Celtic, Germanic, Hellenic, Indo-Iranian and Romance languages are descended from it. This is why many of the words used in Old English bear a striking resemblance to words in other languages. For example, the PIE word for 2 'duwo'. The 'd' softens to a 't' in Old Germanic become 'twai', so in Old English we get twa, which became the modern English two, the modern Swedish två, the modern German zwei and the modern Icelandic tveir.

5th to 6th Century

The grammatical shift known as i-mutation took place separately in various Germanic languages from around 450 or 500 AD. I-mutation is a regular change in the vowels stems of words under certain circumstances, for example, in Modern English we have 'a man' but 'many men', you have 'a tooth' and 'many teeth'. Old English had the same mutation, sometimes for words which are now regular: boc - book but bec - books. However, it is especially important in regards to the formation of weak verbs. For example, if the final consonant in the stem of a Class I Weak Verb is 'bb' or 'cg', it will geminate to 'f' or 'g' respectively. For example, ic lecge - I lay becomes 'ic legde' due to this grammatical rule. This is explained in more detail in the Weak Verbs module.

7th Century

Cædmon's Hymn is a short eulogistic poem praising the Creator of heaven and earth, attributed to Cædmon, an illiterate cow-herd employed by the monastery of Whitby. The poem is written in alliterative verse and was composed sometime between 658 and 680. It is considered to be the oldest surviving non-runic record of Old English poetry, being recorded in the early part of the 8th century and surviving in 19 verified manuscript witness, all of which are copies of the Historia Ecclesiastica.

8th Century

The Ruthwell Cross is a stone cross probably dating from the 7th or 8th century, when the village of Ruthwell, Scotland, was part of the Anglo-Saxon Kingdom of Northumbria. A runic inscription surrounds the carving on one side of the cross, though it is widely believed these runes were added later, possibly as late as the 10th century. The runes are distinct from Norse Futhark, and are generally referred to as Anglo-Saxon runes.
