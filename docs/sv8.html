Class VII Strong Verbs

Class VII verbs are the most irregular. They can have an (a/o/ea/æ/e + consonant). For example, 'hatan - to order/be called', 'blawan - to blow', 'hleapan - to leap' or 'growan - to grow'. When they conjugate, they either they take an 'ē' or an 'ēo' in both the past singular and past plural. For example, examine the verbs 'hatan - to order' and 'cnawan - to know' in the sentences below.

Because of the wide range of verbs that fall into this class, it is the most commonly encountered. Many of the words should be recognisable to an English speaker, and they have generally kept their vowel change, though usually it has been simplified to a single 'e'. For example, 'grow' and 'grew', 'leap' and 'lept', 'know' and 'knew' or 'fall' and 'fell'.

Don't forget, when a 'þ/ð' comes after a vowel and an unvoiced consonant like 't' it becomes a 't' also. So 'hatan' conjugates to 'heo hætt - she orders' not 'heo hætþ'. Similiarly, if a 'þ' comes after a vowel and a 'd', it becomes 't'. For example, 'rædan' becomes 'heo rætt - she advises', not 'heo rætþ'.
