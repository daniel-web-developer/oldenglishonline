Additional Verb Forms Overview

In this module you've gained a more complete picture of Old English verbs, as well as their relationship to modern English. We may not have an inflected infinitive any more, but our present and past participles can be mapped onto their Old English ancestors which were used in some of the same ways, and our many verbs - from unlock, to bear forth - can be seen to have evolved from Old English prefixed verbs.

Inflected Infinitive

The inflected infinitive is a form of the infinitive following the preposition 'to' and ending in -enne, and it can usually be translated as 'in order to [plus infinitive]'. If it follows the verb 'to be' is usually best translated with a verb of oblication such as 'must', 'should', or 'ought' to. The wedding vows 'to have and to hold' would be to hæbbenne ond to healdenne'.

Participles

Participles are verb forms that are defined by their use as adjectives, but that are also used frequently in the formation of verbal constructions such as auixilliary + participle, or 'to be' + participle. Old English didn't have as complex a tense system as modern English (in fact, it really only had past and present forms), but we still find constructions such as 'ten hæfþ - have eaten' and 'wæs geslegen - was slain'.

In Old English the present participle is usually formed with the addition of -ende to the root of the verb, and the past participle is usually formed with the addition of -ed or -od and sometimes with the addition of the prefix ge-. There are some exceptions, as there are in modern English - so the past participle of gan - to go is (ge)gan, and strong verbs usually have an further vowel change to signal the past participle.

A final thing to note with participles, is that when they function as adjectives modifying nouns, they take endings just like any other adjective. The example below 'mid þæm singendan biscope' - 'with the the singing bishop', in which the present participle singende takes the weak adjective ending -an.

Prefixes

Commonly recognised prefixes are used in both modern and Old English to alter the meaning of a base verb in regular ways. So, un- is used in modern English in much the same way as on- was used in Old English - to reverse the meaning of the verb it is attached to. This is one way of forming 'new' verbs as well: the verb 'unfriend' uses two Old English roots, but is only attested in the later medieval period: 'unfriend' in the Facebook sense is repurposing a very old term!

Some important prefixes to look out for are ge- which intensifies or perfects (emphasises completion of) the action; be- which means 'around' or 'about'; and to- which carries the meaning 'break apart'.
