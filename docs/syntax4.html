Syntax Overview

In this module you have learned a few important features of Old English syntax: the structure of clauses and sentences. Syntax is a large topic, and we've really just scratched the surface here. But understanding a little about Word Order, Correlations, and other features of Prose Style should allow you to tackle original texts such as the one below that ends this module.

Word Order

We learned that word order in Old English is more flexible than in modern English due to the relatively extensive system of inflections. Subject-Verb-Object is still the most common order, but Verb-Subject order occurs regularly in clauses introduced by adverbs such as þa - then or nu - now. In relative clauses introduced by the relative pronoun 'þe - which/that/who', the verb usually goes to the end of the clause. These three word orders are illustrated in the sentence below:'Se wicing eode to his scipe, þe on Temese muþan wæs, and þa ferde he to Lundene - the Viking went to his ship, which was in the Thames estuary, and then he travelled to London'. Correlation

One common feature of Old English prose is the use of correlating pairs - words that work together to structure a sentence. Some of the most common are þa...þa (meaning 'then...when', or 'when...then'), and swa...swa (sometimes meaning as...as). With correlating pairs such as þa...þa, the adverb 'then' can be distinguished from the conjunction 'when' based on word order: after 'then', the verb will come before the subject, as in þa se biscop to Eoferwic com, þa forþferde he - when the bishop came to York, (then) he died'.

Old English Prose Style

Finally, we looked at some features of Old English prose style, including a tendency not to restate the subject, the stacking of successive clauses introduced by the conjunction 'ond', as well as recapping on the use of adverbial phrases. There's lots more to learn about Old English syntax, but this will give you a good start. And remember that these are not hard and fast rules - you'll come across Subject-Verb word order where you expect a reversal - so this is just a guide!
