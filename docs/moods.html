Introduction to Mood

One feature of Old English verbs that is not common in modern English, (though common in other languages) is the existence of different inflections - or endings - of the verb to show mood. Mood doesn't refer to whether the subject of the verb is angry or sad; it is a grammatical feature used to signal the intention of the verb: whether it is expressing a command, say, or a desire, or simply a statement of fact.

Indicative Mood

The usual mood of verbs is referred to as the indicative, and the vast majority of verbs you come across in Old English - or in any language - will be in this mood. The indicative mood is often defined as a statement of fact - this does not necessarily mean that a statement is 'true', only that it is presented as a known fact. It includes declarative statements such as 'that horse is brown', express opinions such as 'I think that horse is beautiful' and to ask questions such as 'what is that horse's name?' All the verbs you've come across so far on the course have been in this factual indicative mood.

Imperative and Subjunctive Mood

In modern English we can distinguish additional moods, or intentions, in verbal phrases. For example, 'Get out of bed!' is not a statement of fact, it is a command. This we call the imperative mood, and in Old English there are different verb forms to express this mood. Also, if someone says 'I will go for a walk if it is sunny', they are not stating a fact, but something that might happen and which is conditional on something else happening - the sun coming out. Whilst in modern English the conditional is not usually expressed using different verb forms, many speakers of English would still say 'If I were rich...' using an alternative form of the verb 'to be' to express the conditional. In Old English, it is very common for verb inflections to express this conditional mood, which we call the subjunctive. In the table below you can see examples of these three moods in modern English.
