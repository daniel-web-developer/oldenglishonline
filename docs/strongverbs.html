Introduction to Strong Verbs

In addition to Weak Verbs and Irregular Verbs, Old English also has a category of verbs called Strong Verbs. Unlike Weak Verbs, which form their past tense by adding a -d or -t before the suffix, Strong Verbs form their past tense by changing their root vowel. For example, examine the verb in the following sentences: 'He drincþ of ðæm wine - He drinks the wine' and 'He dranc of ðæm wine - He drank the wine'.

Many verbs in modern English have retained this conjugation pattern, like 'sing/sang' or 'give/gave'. In Old English, there are seven classes of strong verb, each identifiable by specific patterns of letters in the root verb, and each with its own conjugation pattern. Some of these changes can be complex, so let's explore Strong Verbs class by class.
