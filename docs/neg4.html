Negations Overview

Negations in Old English are straightforward, but there are a few things that set them apart from negations in modern English.

Word Order

The negative particle always precedes the verb, and auixlliary verbs like 'do' are not routinely used in negative constructions, as they are in modern English ('I do not like', 'I cannot come', etc). Negations often start a sentence or clause, so we often see constructions such as 'Ne com heo' - 'She didn't come' (literally 'not come she')' or 'Ne stel Ã°u' - 'Do not steal!' (literally 'no steal you!').

Negated Verbs

Sometimes the negative particle that precedes the verb ('ne' in the examples above) is joined to the start of the verb. This phenomenon only happens with certain verbs, the most important being 'wesan' - 'to be', 'habban' - 'to have', and 'willan' - 'to wish/want'. When this fusing of the negative particle to the start of the verb happens, sometimes the initial letters of the verb or the vowel in 'ne' is lost, leaving forms such as 'nis' (a contraction of 'ne is' - 'is not') or 'noldon' (a contraction of 'ne woldon' - 'did not want'). This process also happens with certain indefinite pronouns and adjectives which give us forms such as 'nan' - 'none' (from 'ne an' - 'not one').

Double Negatives

In modern English, negatives cancel each other out - so 'I don't not love Old English' means that you, in fact, do love it. In Old English, double negation simply emphasises the negation, and it is common to find negation applied to every word that can be made negative in a single sentence.
