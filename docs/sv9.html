Strong Verbs Overview

Strong Verbs are one of the most complex elements of Old English grammar as they are so heavily altered by sound changes that happened as Old English evolved from the earliest forms of the language. The important thing to remember about Strong Verbs is that they form their past tense by changing the root vowel of the verb. So 'ic bite - I bite' becomes 'ic bat - I bit' and 'ic write - I write' becomes 'ic wrat - I wrote'.

As you can see, many modern English verbs still form their past tense in this way. However, many verbs which are strong in Old English have weakened in modern English and form their past by adding 'ed' so do not rely on modern verbs to figure out which are strong in Old English. For example, examine the sentences below: 'Wyrm eowre lichaman cywð - Worms chew your body' and 'wyrm eowre lichaman ceaw - Worms chewed your body'.

There are seven classes of Strong Verb, each with a specific signature root and conjugation pattern. So Class II verbs always have an (eo + consonant) in the root. For example, 'beowan - to chew' or 'fleogan - to fly'. While Class VI verbs can have an (a + consonant or e/ie + double consnant). For example, 'wascan - to wash' or 'sceððan - to injure'.

Class Root Signature Change Pattern
I i + consonant i - a - i
II eo + consonant eo - ea - u
III u + consonant i - a - u
IV e + l/r/m/n e - æ - æ
V e + consonant (not l/r/m/n) e - æ - æ
VI a + consonant or e/ie + double consnant a - o - o
VII a/o/ea/æ/e + consonant ea - eo - eo

Rules to remember

I-mutation occurs in most Strong Verbs in the 2nd and 3rd person singular present. So 'Ic cweþe - I say' becomes 'Þu cwist - you say' and 'heo cwiþ - she says'. Do not get confused between the root vowel change when a verb moves to the past tense, 'heo cwæþ - she said' and i-mutation in the present.

Verners law causes voiceless stops to change to a voiced stop if it is not the first or final letter, next to other voiceless consonants, or not immediately proceeded by the stress. This means that when verbs with letters like 'þ' directly after the vowel conjugate, the 'þ' changes to a d and s changes r.

Double consonants If a difficult to pronounce double consonant would be produced by conjugation, it will be simplified so that it is easier to pronounce. So if a 'þ/ð' comes after an unvoiced consonant like 't', it becomes a 't' also. So 'hatan' conjugates to 'heo hætt - she orders' not 'heo hætþ'. Similiarly, if a 'þ' comes after a 'd', they become 'tt'. For example, 'rædan' becomes 'heo rætt - she advises', not 'heo rætþ'.
