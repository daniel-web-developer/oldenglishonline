Letters of the Old English Alphabet

English minuscule varied over time and between scribes. Below you will find descriptions and variants for each letter form found in Old English manuscripts.

The most common ‘a’ found in Old English is the insular 'a', but its form varied significantly as English minuscule evolved. In pointed minuscule (1, 2), the ‘a’ has a sharply sloped stroke to the arch of the letter. Square minuscule (3,4) has a square aspect and almost resembles the form of ‘u’, as it is often composed of two minims with flicked serifs closing the counter. Round minuscule (4,5,6) has a curved bow and a straight back with a flicked serif, but be aware that round minuscule ‘a’ can sometimes also have a sloped stroke to the arch of the letter so pay attention to the general aspect of the letter and hand.

Caroline ‘a’ is more commonly found in the Latin sections of English manuscripts, however they do occasionally creep up in the Old English sections. The caroline ‘a’ is distinguished by a rounded bowl and a tall back with a pronounced leading stroke creating a horn.

Half uncial a’s are formed with a round curved bow and a curved back. They are generally found in Latin sections; not Old English. For example, the Latin sections of Cotton MS Nero D IV are written in half uncial, however in MS Bodlein Junius II, the half-uncial ‘a’ appears in the Old English alongside the usual insular ‘a’. The half uncial ‘a’ can look like an ‘ac’ or a poorly formed ash, so be aware that this letter form exists.

In Irish insular minuscule there is an open ‘a’ with two horns. This very rarely shows up in Old English manuscripts but can be found in the Old English glosses in the Lindisfarne Gospels.

The letter ‘ash’ is a is composed of an 'a' and 'e'. In most hands, the bow of the ‘e’ protrudes slightly higher than the ‘a’ (1,2), though there are numerous examples of the forms being the same height (3,4). However, a form which may be confusing is an ash with a ‘tall e’ (5,6). For these the loop of the ‘e’ extends far above the arch of the ‘a’. Tall e’s are commonly used when the scribe wishes to make a ligature with an adjoining letter. Compare the two ash in (7). The tall ‘s’ does not have a head stroke while the ‘t’ does, so the scribe adjusts the type of ‘e’ to create a ligature which joins the cross stroke of the ‘e’ and the head stroke of the ‘t’.

The letter 'e' is generally consistent between hands throughout the early medieval period and resembles the modern character 'e'. In many scripts, the cross-stroke of the ‘e’ often starts before the back and extends beyond the letter, occasionally functioning as a ligature. You will occasionally see 'tall e' on its own, however this is more common when writing Latin. For example, in BL Harley 585, the scribe only uses 'tall e' when writing Latin, and uses them interchangably with regular 'e'.

The letters 'b', 'l', and 'h' generally have short approach strokes and wedge-shaped finials on the ascenders. The shoulder of the H is rounded with a small foot. The bow on the B is round and closed, touching the stem both at the top and bottom of the curve. The L is composed of a single stroke with a short, curved foot.

The letters 't', 'c', and 'o' change little as the script evolved, and are recognisably similar to their equivelant modern characters. The T is comprised of a flat head stroke and a curved stem. C is composed of a single curved stroke. O is composed of a single circular stroke which creates a closed counter.

The letters 'd' and 'eth' generally have noticably distinct letter forms within the same hand. The stroke that comprises the ascender of 'd' is usually short, thick and rounded over the bowl of the 'd', while the ascender of the 'eth' is long and curves upwards towards the tip. Even when the ascender of the 'd' is long, the ascender of an 'eth' is usually longer. The cross-stroke of the 'eth' is thin and often hooks downwards on one side. Double Eth’s in words like 'siððan' share a single cross-stroke.

Capital 'eth' resembles a modern D, with a straight stem, a curved bow, and a short cross stroke through the stem. This cross stroke often has serifs on either end, but not always.

There can be a significant amount of variance in how scribes approaches the letter G, with the tail either being open or closed, and the neck originating on the left, centre or right, and the form itself being composed of two strokes — one for the head-stroke and one for the body — or a single continuous pen stroke.

Thorns have a long stem with a wedge shaped finial on the ascender, and the descender tapering to the left towards the end of the stroke. The bow is rounded and usually closed. Thorns are most commonly used as an initial letter. As a result, many thorns are elaborately decorated ().

While the letterforms of P and Wynn are similar, there are a few notable distinctions. The approach stroke of the P is short and is part of the stroke that comprises the stem, while the approach stroke of the Wynn is longer and is part of the stroke that comprises the bowl. The curve of the bowl in the P is round and closed with the thinner part of the stroke connected to the upper part of the stem, while the curve of the Wynn tapers downwards where the lower part of the curve attaches to the stem. The aspect of the P is broader than the Wynn. Like, initial thorns, initial wynns are often decorated ().

S

There are three forms of 's' found in Old English. The first is referred to as a 'low s', the second a 'tall s', and the last a 'round s'. The round 's' is what is used in modern English. However, it is not uncommon to find all three 's' in use by a single scribe in a single manuscript.

In some hands, the split between the stem and shoulder of the S can be quite deep, making it difficult to distinguish between Y and S in cases where the Y is not dotted. Low s is also visually similar to a modern 'r' and 'tall s' is visually similar to a modern 'f' so make sure you do not confuse the two.

I U M N

The I’s are composed of a single minim. M and N are composed of three and two minims respectively, joined by a rounded shoulder. As with the minims in other letters, the approach-strokes and feet are short are short on each letter.
