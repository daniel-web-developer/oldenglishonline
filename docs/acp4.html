Prepositions

Prepositions are words that show how a verb relates to a noun or pronoun. Most Old English prepositions are still recognisable to a modern English reader, as prepositions have changed very little as the language has evolved. However, prepositions in Old English govern which case the following noun or pronoun takes. Up until now, almost all nouns paired with a preposition have taken the dative case. However, depending on which preposition is used, a noun can also take the accusative or genitive case.

The following prepositions usually preceed the dative case, though some can take the accusative in specific situations.

There are four prepositions which only precede the accusative case. These are 'geond - through', 'oð - until', 'þurh - through' and 'ymb - surrounding/about'.

Some prepositions could be considered false friends, as they look like one word but represent another. For example, the word 'æt' means 'to' and the word 'wiþ' means against. It is common to see 'wiþ' used at the beginning of medical instructions in leechbooks to indicate what the recipe is used for, in which case it might be more correct to translate it as 'for'. For example, 'wiþ aslepnum lic - for a numb body' or 'wiþ nædran flite - for a snake bite'.
