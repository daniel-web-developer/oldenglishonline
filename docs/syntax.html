Syntax: The Basics

If grammar is defined as a set of rules governing how a language works, syntax is that particular branch of grammar dealing with the formation of clauses and sentences - bringing words and phrases together to create meaning. For example, take the sentence 'The children left the house when the vikings came to town'. We can understand the individual elements - that the verb is in the past tense; that the subject is plural - but we also have to consider how these elements work together. One of the ways we can analyse the sentence structure is to divide it into two clauses, the main clause and the subordinate clause.

The children left the house
Main Clause
when the vikings came to town
Subordinate clause

We know that the clause 'The children left the house' is a statement that can stand on its own, but that 'when the vikings came' cannot - it needs to be completed by the main clause.

Word Order

We can rearrange the main clause and the subordinate clause, and the sentence still makes sense - 'When the vikings came to town, the children left the house', but if we rearrange the nouns within the sentence the meaning is drastically altered: 'When the children left the house, the vikings came to town'. If we rearrange the words within the clauses, the result is gobbledygook: 'the house left the children, came to town when the vikings'. Note that the basic morphology - the form of the words - hasn't changed. Word order is particularly important in modern English, as it is what we call a synthetic language. It has few inflections, but word order and prepositions have to do a lot of work in the sentence.

Old English has more inflections indicating the grammar of individual words and how they relate to one another, so it can be more flexible with word order. Examine the sentence below, Þa þa wicingas to tune comon, þa forleton þa bearn þæt hus, which literally translates as 'When the vikings to town came, then left the children the house' - a word order which would create confusion in modern English.

Þa þa wicingas to tune comon
Subordinate clause
þa forleton þa bearn þæt hus
Main Clause

Correlation

One thing that sets Old English prose apart from modern English is the frequent use of correlative pairs: two elements in a sentence that depend on one another. Some examples of this in modern English are 'either...or', 'neither...nor' and 'not only...but also'. When we see 'either the cat goes...' in a sentence, we expect a clause introduced by or ('or I go...') to follow. Old English has a number of these pairings that work together in sentences, and we'll look at a few of the most common examples. One of them appears in the sentence above - þa...þa, translated here as 'when...then'.

Old English Prose Style

There are some further quirks of Old English sentences - particularly the more complex ones - that makes them feel a little alien even when we recognise the individual clauses. For example, look at the rather long sentence below, and the frequent use of the conjunction 'ond - and'': Þa gecierede se weard to Criste ond awearp his hrægl him of ond hleop on ðone mere ond stod on ðara midle ond his gast to gode onsænde - Then the guard converted to Christ and threw his clothes off, and leaped into the lake, and stood in their midst, and sent up his spirit to god
