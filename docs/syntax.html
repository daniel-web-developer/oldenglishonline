Syntax: The Basics

If grammar is defined as a set of rules governing how a language works, syntax is that particular branch of grammar dealing with the formation of clauses and sentences - bringing words and phrases together to create meaning. For example, take the sentence 'They left the town when the vikings came'. We can understand the individual elements - that the verb is in the past tense; that the subject is plural - but we also have to consider how these elements work together. One of the ways we can analyse the sentence structure is to divide it into two clauses, the main clause and the subordinate clause.

Word Order

We can rearrange the main clause and the subordinate clause, and the sentence still makes sense - 'When the vikings came, the people left the towm', but if we rearrange the words within the sentence the meaning is drastically altered: 'When the people came, the vikings left the town'. If we rearrange the words within the clauses, the result is gobbledygook: 'Town the left people, came when the vikings'. Note that the basic morphology - the form of the words - hasn't changed. Word order is particularly important in modern English, as it is what we call a synthetic language. It has few inflections, but word order and prepositions do a lot of work in the sentence.

Old English has more inflections indicating the grammar of individual words and clauses, so it can be more flexible with word order. We will learn more about this in the next lesson.

Correlation

One thing that sets Old English prose apart from modern English is the frequent use of correlative pairs: two elements in a sentence that depend on one another. Some examples of this in modern English are 'either...or', 'neither...nor' and 'not only...but also'. When we see 'either he goes...' in a sentence, we expect 'orI go...'. Old English has a number of these pairings that work together in sentences, and we'll look at a few of the most common examples.

Quirks of Old English Style

There are some further quirks of Old English sentences - particularly the more complex ones - that makes them feel a little alien. For example, look at the rather long sentence below, and the frequent use of the conjunction ond - and: Þa gecierede se weard to Criste ond awearp his hrægl him of ond hleop on ðone mere ond stod on ðara midle ond his gast to gode onsænde - Then the guard converted to Christ and threw his clothes off, and leaped into the lake, and stood in their midst, and sent up his spirit to god
