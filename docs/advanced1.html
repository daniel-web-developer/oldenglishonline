Modal Auxiliary Verbs

Modal verbs are a particular class of auxiliary verbs that express necessity, possibility, intention, or ability. The verbs we use today - including must, should and can - are almost all descended from Old English. First we need to make clear what distinguishes auxiliary verbs from normal verbs.

Auxiliary Verbs

Auxiliary verbs are 'helping' verbs that combine with the main verb to make meaning. For example, in modern English we use auxiliary verbs such as 'to have' to form various tenses, and auxiliaries such as 'to be' to express the passive voice (for example, in the sentence 'the window was broken'). Look at the sentences below and make sure you can identify the auxiliary verb and the main verb.

They should not be here!

What will you do?

I really must practice more

Modals

The modal verbs in modern English are must, shall, will, should, would, can, could, ought (to), may, and might. They are used with a main verb to express an obligation (I must go), and intention (I will go), an ability (I can go), a permission (I may go), and so on. Some Old English modal auxiliaries are listed in the table below: see if you can see where the modern English modals come from in each case.

Modal Auxiliary Verbs
Old English Modern English 3rd Present 3rd Past
magan to be able to / can mæg meahte
sculan to be supposed to / should sceal scolde
cunnan to know how to / can cann cuþe
motan to be allowed / can / must mot moste
willan to intend / be willing to wille wolde

In simple constructions modal auxiliaries are followed by a verb in the infinitive (as in modern English 'I should eat/leave/run'). To recap, the infinitive is the base form of a verb, which ends in -an in Old English. Modal auxiliary + infinitive constructions such as 'Ic cann rune writan - I can write runes' or 'Heo mot singan - She must sing' behave in exactly the same way as in modern English: the modal verb is followed by the main verb in the infinitive.

Auxiliary + Infinitive Word Order

One thing worth looking out for when encountering modal auxiliaries is that the infinitive verb that follows often goes to the very end of the clause or sentence. The key thing it to remember that when a modal (or any auxiliary verb) is being used, you should look for the verb that it is helping. For example, in the sentence 'Cyning sceal on healle beagas dælan - A king should distribute rings in the hall', the auxiliary sceal needs a main verb, which in this case has gone after the direct and indirect objects.

Finally, it is worth mentioning that although the irregular verb 'to do' was used regularly in Old English, it was not used as an auixiliary in the formation of questions or negations as it is in modern English. So we would find 'He swa dyde - he did so', but 'Dranc he?rather than 'did he drink?' and 'ne dranc he' rather than 'he didn't drink'.

Old English modal auxiliaries are straightforward due to their similarity to the modern English descendants. Something that Old English has, but modern English lacks is the inflected infinitive, dealt with next.
