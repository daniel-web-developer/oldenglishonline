Advanced Verbs

To recap, the infinitive is the base form of the verb. In Old English, infinitives end in -an. When verbs are inflected, the -an ending is removed, and the inflection is added: for example, the infinitive of the Old English verb 'to flow' is 'flowan'; the root is flow-; and the present singular inflection/ending -e is added to give us 'Ic flowe - I flow'.

In addition to being the 'dictionary form' of the verb, equivalent to modern English 'to flow', the infinitive can also be used following an auxiliary or inflected verb for example in the sentence 'He het streamas flowan - He ordered streams to flow'. The auxiliary verb + infinitive is a very common construction, as it is in modern English, and here the infinitive flowan can be translated directly by the modern English infinitive 'to flow'.

However, in Old English we also come across what is known as an inflected infinitive. This inflection follows the preposition 'to', and rather than the infinitive form, the ending -enne is added. So the inflected infinitive of 'hieran - to hear' is 'to hierenne'. Because this inflection can be used for emphasis, it is often best translated as 'in order to', in this case 'in order to hear''. The inflected infinitive also sometimes follows the verb 'beon - to be', and here it usually expresses some kind of obligation, and can be translated using 'must', 'should' or 'ought'. See the examples below, both using the inflected infinitive of the verb 'healdan - to keep or hold: 'Hie timbrodon byrig, þæt land to healdenne - They built forts in order to hold the land' and 'Eow is þearf daglic fasten to healdenne - - 'It is very needful for you to hold daily fasts'.

When you come across inflected infinitives, look at the context to see how they should best be translated. Often 'in order to' is the best translation, but use the rest of the sentence to help you out.
