Correlation

Something you will find very often in Old English prose is the use of correlating elements joining together clauses. Examples of these correlating pairs in modern English are 'either...or', 'neither...nor', 'both...and', 'not only...but also', 'rather...than', and so on. Examine the sentence below, 'You either love it, or you hate it' - the use of either requires or to open the next clause in order to complete the sentence.

You

either
love it,

or
you hate it

Correlative elements like this can be thought of as sidekicks - when you see Batman, you can be sure that Robin is following closely behind. Most of the time, correlative elements in modern English connect independent clauses of equal weight, such as in the sentence above. Some correlations in Old English work like this, and some introduce relative or subordinate clauses. Here are some correlations that you are likely to come across.

þa...þa

This is the most frequently used correlation in Old English. þa is a tricky little word, as it is both a demonstrative pronoun (nominative and accusative plural, and feminine accusative singular), an adverb meaning 'then', and a conjunction meaning 'when'. 'þa...þa'' also works as a correlative pair meaning 'then...when' or 'when...then'. Look at the sentence below, and see if you can spot a way that we might be able to tell them apart: 'Ða se cyng ðæt hierde, þa wende he west - When the king heard that, then he turned himself west'.

The position of the verb is the key thing for working out which is which. When þa starts a clause and is the adverb 'then', the verb will come before the subject, as in 'þa wende he hine west'. When þa starts a clause and is the conjunction 'when', the verb comes after the subject, and often goes to the end of the clause, as in 'Ða se cyng ðæt hierde'.

You also find þa þa used sequentially, to mean 'then when'. The sentence below, þa þa he slæpte þa genam he an rib of Adames sidan - then when he slept, (then) he took one rib from Adam's side' has both a þa þa construction, and a þa introducing the next clause and meaning 'then'.

There are a couple of things to note about the sentence above. Firstly, it isn't really necessary to include the second þa in the translation - in modern English it would be redundant. Secondly, note that the verb comes before the subject in the second clause - meaning that 'þa genam he' means 'then he took'. Finally, whilst there may be confusion about the subject 'he' in each clause meaning different people, the context makes it very clear that the first he is Adam, and the second he is God!

A final thing to note: sometimes you'll find þa written with a thorn, and sometimes as ða, sometimes even in the same sentence! Also, consecutive clauses with þa might be a sequence of 'then' statements: watch where the verb is to be sure!

You will also come across the correlation ðonne...ðonne: this works in the same way as þa þa, and also means 'then...when' or 'when...then'.

þær...þær

This correlation funtions in much the same way as þa...þa above. þær on its own can mean either 'there' or 'where'. When it is the adverb 'there', the verb comes before the subject (just as with þa when it functions as the adverb 'then'). When þær means 'where', the verb will come after the subject. þær...þær can also be used sequentially, meaning 'there where...'. In these cases, we can usually drop 'there' in translation, or put it in brackets to show that we know it is there in the original, but that it isn't needed in modern English.

Examine the sentence below, 'Þær þær þæt gemynd bið, þær bið þæt andgyt - (There) where memory is, there is (the) understanding

Note that in the sentence above, we have the normal subject-verb order after the clause beginning 'there where', and a reversal (verb-subject) in the second clause beginning with the adverb 'there'. Also, we probably wouldn't include the first 'there' in a translation, 'where memory is, there is understanding'.

Note also that the verb used is not the more common is (from wesan - to be) but bið (from beon - to be). Remember that beon is used to express permanent or general truths: that's why it's used here.

Swa...swa

Swa...swa works a little differently to the correlative pairs introduced so far. swa on its own can be an adverb meaning 'so' or 'very', or a conjunction meaning 'as'. But it's often found as a correlative meaning 'the...the', as a comparative meaning 'as...as', or sequentially meaning '(just) as' or '(just) like'. This use is found in a very well known text - the Lord's Prayer - in the expression 'on eorðan swa swa on heofonum - on earth just as in heaven':

Oðer...oðer

The Old English term oðer/oþer is another versatile word. It can function as an ordinal number meaning 'second'; it can be an indefinite pronoun meaning 'one', or it can be an adjective meaning 'other'. It is also used in a correlating construction which usually means 'one...the other'. Examine the sentence below, 'Lamech nam twa wif; oðer wæs genemned Ada ond oðer Sella - Lamech took two wives; one was named Ada and the other Sella.

As you can see, when these two words appear together within a sentence, they mean something different than when they appear on their own. The key with correlating pairs is that they work together to structure sentences, just like modern English 'either...or' or 'not only...but also'. You'll find them used a lot in Old English to structure sentences - prose writers were clearly very fond of them!

Ge...ge

You've come across ge as a second person personal pronoun (meaning 'ye' or 'y'all'). But it is aslo a conjunction meaning 'both' or 'and'. It is frequently used as correlative pair, meaning 'both...and'. Look at this final example to finish, in which a succession of ge conjunctions are used to structure the sentence, each translated in a slightly different way to give the same effect in modern English: 'God wat ealle þing, ge þæt gedon is, ge þæt þe nu is, ge þæt ðe toweard is - God knows everything, both that which is done, that which now is, and that which is coming'.
