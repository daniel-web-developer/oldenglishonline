Nouns Overview

In Old English there are three major categories of nouns: Strong, Weak, and Minor. Of the three, Strong Nouns are the most numerous, with Minor Nouns being the least common. Nouns never change their strength. A Strong noun, like 'scip' is always strong, a Weak noun like 'tunge' is always weak, and a Minor noun like 'sunu' is always minor.

Strong Nouns

Strong nouns are considered grammatically strong because there are many variations in how they decline based on gender, case, and number. Strong nouns are typically subdivded into three 'genders' called Masculine, Neuter, and Feminine.

Though there are more variations in Strong nouns than Weak, there are many similarities between the genders which can help you identify which case the noun is in. For example, the demonstrative pronoun 'þæm' always signifies the dative, and the 'um' ending always signifies the dative plural. Similarly, the demonstrative pronoun 'þara' and the suffix 'a', always signify the genitive plural. While the dative declensions for masculine, neuter and feminine nouns are identical, you only ever see the demonstrative pronoun 'þære' with feminine nouns.

Weak Nouns

Weak nouns are considered grammatically weak because, unlike Strong nouns, there are very few variations in how they decline. Only the nominative singular and neuter accusative forms differ between genders, and there are only two weak neuter nouns: 'eage - eye' and 'eare - ear'. All other nouns are either masculine or feminine.

Since Weak nouns decline almost identically between genders, and have simliar inflectional suffix' between cases, often demonstrative pronouns are the best way of identifying a noun's gender and case. Where a demonstrative pronoun is not present, you must rely on the context of the noun to figure out its case.

Minor Nouns

Minor nouns are the least numerous, often having only a handful of nouns per category. They can be broken down into several sub-categories: u-declensions, r-plurals, family nouns, and i-mutation nouns.

U-Declensions

Unlike Strong and Weak nouns, nouns in the u-declension decline the same for both masculine and feminine. There are no neuter nouns in the u-declension.

R-Plurals

R-plurals are nouns which add an 'r' between the stem and the suffix when declining in the plural. All r-plural nouns are neuter and, in all other ways, the nouns decline normally as per the rules of strong neuter nouns. There are four nouns in the r-plural category: 'lamb - lamb', 'æg - egg', 'cealf - calf', and 'cild - child'.

R-Plural Declension

Accusative cild cildra
Genitive cildes cildra

Family Nouns

The family nouns are 'fæder', 'modor', 'broðor', 'sweostor', and 'dohtor'. They are the most irregular of the minor declensions.
