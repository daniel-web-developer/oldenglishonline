Old English Language

A Quick Note On The Alphabet Before You Begin

While Old English is no longer spoken, understanding how to pronounce the words you read will help you remember them and understand the text better.

Old English has no silent letters, so you should pronounce every letter in a word. For example, the ‘c’ in cnoll — summit, is pronounced as a hard c, unlike the modern English equivelant ‘knoll’. The ‘e’ in here — army is pronouned making the word sound more like ‘her-eh’ than the modern English ‘here’.

Old English was first written in futhorc runes, but from the 9th century onwards it began to be transcribed into a Latin alphabet. The Old English alphabet is:
A B C D E F G H I K L M N O P Q R S T U X Y ⁊ Ƿ Þ Ð Æ Ᵹ

Old English letters are largely pronounced the same way we pronounce them in modern English, however, below you will find a list of letters unique to Old English.


Old English Runic Letters
Letter Modern English IPA Name
Ƿ / ƿ W (as in win) [w] Wynn
Ð / ð TH (as in thing) [ð] Eth
Þ / þ TH (as in thick) [θ], [ð] Thorn
Æ / æ AE (as in cat) [æː] Ash
Ᵹ / ᵹ G, Y or J [g], [ɣ], [ʎ], [j] Insular G / Yogh
⁊ And or & - Tironan notae or et

You’ll notice the letters V, W and J are missing from the Old English alphabet. While the letter V existed in the time of the Anglo-Saxons, it was used almost exclusively when writing Latin. The letter W is replaced in Old English by the letter Wynn ( Ƿ), and the letter J is replaced with a G. Just as with the modern English ‘G’, Ᵹ can express multiple sounds. For example, a hard ‘g’, as in glæd — glad; a soft ‘g’ which sounds similar to ‘y’, for example, gīet — yet or þegen — ‘thane’. It can even function as a a ‘zh’ sound when it appears after ‘n’, engel — angel

Note: To make Old English easier to read and understand, Ƿ and Ᵹ will be replaced by 'w' ‘g’ in most of the practice material.

It should also be noted that, when found in sequence, the letters sc are usually pronounced as ‘sh'. For example, scip is pronounced ‘ship’, and fisc as ‘fish’. However, there are a few exceptions to this rule, for example āscian — ‘ask’.

Remember: When in doubt, pronounce it like you would in modern English. So long as you remember to pronounce every letter, how we pronounce the word in modern English is usually a good guide on how to pronounce in Old English.
