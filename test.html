Nominative and Genitive Strong Nouns

The nominative and genitive refer to the form of a noun used for the subject of a sentence and the form used for possession. In þæs cyninges þegen - The King’s thane - the thane belongs to the king so þegen is in the nominative and cyninges is in the genitive.

What ending a noun takes is not just determined by its role in a sentence, but also its number; whether it is single or plural. For example, in þæs cyninges biscopas - the king’s bishops - bishop has a different ending than thane.

As you can see from the paradigm above, Old English can be quite similar to modern English. The nominative form of Cyning, Biscop and Þegen all add ‘ as’ to become plural, much like how (s) is added to the end of a word to make it plural in modern English. The singular genitive is made by adding ‘ es’, much like how (‘s) is added in English. The plural genitive is the one that looks the most different, in modern English we’d write it as (kings’), adding an apostrophe after the s, while Old English simply adds the suffix ' a'.

The singular form of the nominative is the ‘plain’ form of the word, and it is what you will use to look up words in dictionaries.
