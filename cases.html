Introduction to Old English Cases

Old English, much like many Indo-European languages, is an inflected language. What this means is that endings of nouns, pronouns, and adjectives change depending on their grammatical function, irrespective of where they appear in the sentence.

In modern English, word order is largely what determines the meaning of a word and the word order generally follows the order: subject, verb, object. For example, Dog bites man. In 'dog bites man', dog is the subject (what is performing the action), bites is the verb (the action), and man is the direct object (the object being acted upon). If you reversed the order of the words, man bites dog, the meaning of the sentence is changed.

How a word changes or declines is also affected by its number, for example, whether it is singular or plural, its grammatical gender, and it's grammatical strength. Grammatical gender and strength are independant of the meaning of the word and will be explored in more detail in a later module. The declined forms of the same word are called declensions.

There are four major cases:
The Nominative Case indicates the subject of the sentence.
The Accusative Case indicates the direct object of a sentence.
The Genitive Case indicates possession.
The Dative Case indicates the indirect object of a sentence.
