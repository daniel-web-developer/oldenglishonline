Old English Cases


Old English, much like German and Russian, is an inflected language, and as such its nouns, pronouns, adjectives and determiners are declined depending on their grammatical function. A set of declined forms of the same word pattern is called a declension and Old English nouns decline for case, gender, strength, and number.

Cases are changes in the form of a nouns and noun-like words which indicate the role of the word in the sentence. Modern English has only two cases for nouns: a general and possessive, which is used to indicate ownership. Consider: The dog bone vs The dog's bone.

However, Old English has many more. There are five major cases: nominative, accusative, dative, genitive and instrumental.

The nominative case indicates the subject of the sentence, for example: Se cyning means 'the king'. It was also used for direct address. Adjectives in the predicate (qualifying a noun on the other side of 'to be') were also in the nominative.

The accusative case indicates the direct object of the sentence, for example: Æþelbald lufode þone cyning means "Æþelbald loved the king", where Æþelbald is the subject and the king is the object. Already the accusative had begun to merge with the nominative; it was never distinguished in the plural, or in a neuter noun.

The genitive case indicates possession, for example: þæs cyninges scip is "the ship of the king" or "the king's ship". It also indicates partitive nouns.

The dative case indicates the indirect object of the sentence; To whom or for whom the object was meant. For example: hringas þæm cyninge means "rings for the king" or "rings to the king". Here, the word cyning is in its dative form: cyninge. There were also several verbs that took direct objects in the dative.

The instrumental case was somewhat rare and occurred only in the masculine and neuter singular. It was often replaced by the dative. Adjectives, pronouns and (sometimes) participles agreed with their antecedent nouns in case, number and gender. Finite verbs agreed with their subject in person and number.
